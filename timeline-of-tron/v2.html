Chapter One

The Shape of a Life

22 years of sentiment, scored by machine, annotated by hand.
The peaks aren't where you'd expect.

Between 2004 and 2026, John documented 226 milestones. Each one was scored for emotional valence — how positive or negative the language was. Stacked together, they form a shape. Not a success story that climbs steadily upward. Something stranger.

2004 is a pit. The car accident that started the entire project sits at the bottom of the chart, the most negative year in 22 years of recording. And then: a slow, uneven climb. Not toward happiness exactly, but toward something more interesting.

2009 had a sentiment score of only +0.100 — moderate at best. But it recorded the widest emotional range in the entire dataset: 0.998 on a 0-to-1 scale. That year contained ECD's retirement, John's first concert, his first skydive, and WrestleMania 25. His quotes that year swing between "Sometimes it is hell trying to get to heaven" and "You cannot fear death once you've touched heaven." It wasn't his happiest year. It was his most alive year. The greatest years aren't the calmest.

The other surprise: 2023 and 2016 are the emotional peaks — not 2013, which John himself called "the greatest year of my adulthood." The data doesn't always agree with memory. What we remember as great and what we wrote as great are different things.

And at the end, a dip. 2024 and 2025 slide back below zero — the first negative years since 2004. A relationship transition, kidney stones, influenza. The line bends downward. But we've seen this shape before.

The pattern holds for major crises at an 80% rate. For minor ones: 0%. This isn't a guy who turns every bad day into a project. It's specific: when the body genuinely breaks, he redirects the energy into building something. The bigger the break, the bigger the build.

The honest caveat: some of what looks like "creation" is continuation. WWE attendance was already a habit by 2017. Travel was already a pattern. The real signal is in what's new — ECD after the accident, the gym after the mental health crisis, the ring after the surgery. Those weren't planned. They were responses.

Chapter Three

The Writer's Voice

79 quotes across 22 years. What changed isn't what he said —
it's how sure he was when he said it.

In 2007, John wrote: "Without any hesitation, I proudly proclaim that 2007 was undoubtedly the most colorful and eventful year of my life." Count the qualifiers. Without any hesitation. Proudly. Undoubtedly. Most colorful and eventful. A man that sure doesn't need four qualifiers. He's performing confidence.

By 2023, describing what he'd call an even greater experience, he wrote: "the most spectacular day I've ever experienced." Seven words. No qualifiers. No need.

That's the entire story of the voice, in two quotes.

Chapter Four

Who Was There

164 people appear in this dataset. The real story is in who doesn't.

226
milestones documented
66
mention anyone by name
50

That's the entire relationship story, in three numbers.

Of 226 milestones across 22 years, only 29% mention another human being. And of those, 76% mention the same person: Ma. Everyone else — The Pops, Rob, Michael, Megan, Dan Spengeman — appears once or twice.

This isn't isolation. John had 124 people in his ECD community alone. He travels with people, works with people, lives among people. But the documentation is deliberately selective. This is a man who tells the story of his own achievement, not his social life. The people are there. The diary just doesn't always name them.

Ma is the only person who appears in both the highest and lowest emotional moments. Everyone else is context-specific: The Pops appears only in positive contexts (recreation, joy). Rob appears only in crisis contexts. Ma is the only constant across the full emotional spectrum — 20 years, every kind of moment.

And here's what the numbers miss: 2004, the year of the car accident, the most negative year in the dataset — mentions zero people. 2017, the year of spinal surgery — mentions only Ma. John documents positive social transitions with wider circles, but handles personal crises quietly. The harder it gets, the fewer names appear.

Chapter Five

What He Built

ECD wasn't a hobby. It was identity. And the proof is what happened when it ended.

In November 2005, a 20-year-old recovering from a car accident started a dodgeball group. By 2006, it had 51 events in a single year. By 2008, there were 270+ numbered events, 100+ players, a Hall of Fame, documented rivalries, and a blog voice called "The Writer" that published twice a week.

Then it ended. In January 2009, John forced a clean retirement rather than let the community degrade. His quote: "It wasn't something I was happy about doing, but it is something I'm happy I did."

2005–2006
The Founding
20 events in year one, 51 in year two. Born from a car accident's aftermath. Community building as recovery mechanism.
2007–2008
The Golden Age
Peak participation. Hall of Fame system, legendary moments (John in the garbage can, DODGEBOWL 200), rivalries documented with the precision of ESPN.
Jan 2009
The Retirement
"I got tired of kissin' ass." Clean exit after 171 events. Legacy secured rather than degraded. The hardest decision in the dataset.
2010–2017
The Ceremonial Era
2-6 events per year. Annual gatherings, not weekly games. The community became a memory institution.
2021–2025
The Transfer
John's community-building impulse moved to professional senior living work. Self-rating hit 9.8/10 — his highest ever — with ECD dormant. The community still gathers. Next-generation family members attend.
ECD proves something about John that no single chart could: he can create real institutional meaning from nothing. A dodgeball league became a 20-year community that outlasted the sport itself. When health crisis forced an exit, he didn't collapse — he transferred the skill to where it was needed most. The community still gathers in 2025, bringing children who weren't born when it started.
